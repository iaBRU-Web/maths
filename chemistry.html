Section A: Multiple Choice Questions  (30 Marks)

Question 1

Which statement is correct?

  • A. Coordination number is always equal to oxidation number
  • B. Oxidation number depends on ligand denticity
  • C. Coordination number depends on number of donor atoms
  • D. Oxidation number is always positive
Explanation: The coordination number of a central atom in a complex is determined by the number of donor atoms (ligand atoms that donate electron pairs) directly bonded to it.
Question 2

Which species has the greatest number of unpaired electrons?

  • A. Fe²⁺
  • B. Cr
  • C. Cr³⁺
  • D. Mn²⁺
Explanation: Mn²⁺ has configuration [Ar] 3d⁵ — five singly occupied d orbitals, giving 5 unpaired electrons. This is the maximum for first-row transition metal ions listed here. Fe²⁺ → 4 unpaired, Cr³⁺ → 3 unpaired.
Question 3

Which particle has the smallest mass?

  • A. Proton
  • B. Neutron
  • C. Electron
  • D. Nucleus
Explanation: An electron has a mass of approximately 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg, which is about 1/1836 the mass of a proton or neutron.
Question 4

In a mass spectrometer, ions are separated based on:

  • A. Charge only
  • B. Mass/charge ratio
  • C. Velocity
  • D. Density
Explanation: The magnetic field deflects ions according to their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). Lighter and more highly charged ions are deflected more.
Question 5

The electronic configuration of Mg²⁺ is:

  • A. 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²
  • B. 1s²2s²2p⁶
  • C. 1s²2s²2p⁶3s¹
  • D. 1s²2s²2p³
Explanation: Mg is [Ne] 3s². Losing 2 electrons removes both 3s electrons, leaving 1s²2s²2p⁶ — isoelectronic with Neon.
Question 6

Which principle explains orbital filling?

  • A. Pauli exclusion
  • B. Hund's rule
  • C. Aufbau principle
  • D. All of the above
Explanation: All three govern orbital filling — Aufbau (fill lowest energy first), Pauli exclusion (max 2 electrons per orbital, opposite spins), and Hund's rule (maximise unpaired electrons in degenerate orbitals).
Question 7

Ionic bonding involves:

  • A. Sharing electrons
  • B. Transfer of electrons
  • C. Delocalized electrons
  • D. Proton exchange
Explanation: Ionic bonding occurs via complete transfer of one or more electrons from a metal to a non-metal, forming oppositely charged ions that attract electrostatically.
Question 8

Which has the highest melting point?

  • A. NaCl
  • B. CO₂
  • C. CH₄
  • D. NH₃
Explanation: NaCl forms a giant ionic lattice with strong electrostatic forces in all directions. Its melting point is ~801°C. CO₂, CH₄, and NH₃ are simple molecular substances with far weaker intermolecular forces.
Question 9

Which force is strongest?

  • A. London forces
  • B. Dipole-dipole
  • C. Hydrogen bonding
  • D. Covalent bond
Explanation: Covalent bonds are intramolecular (within a molecule) involving shared electron pairs between atoms — significantly stronger than any intermolecular force listed.
Question 10

Atomic radius decreases across a period due to:

  • A. Increased shielding
  • B. Increased nuclear charge
  • C. Fewer electrons
  • D. Lower attraction
Explanation: Across a period, proton count rises while electrons enter the same shell (same shielding). Greater nuclear charge pulls electrons closer, shrinking the atomic radius.
Question 11

Group 1 elements react with water to produce:

  • A. Acid
  • B. Salt only
  • C. Alkali + hydrogen
  • D. Oxygen
Explanation: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑ — products are a metal hydroxide (alkali) and hydrogen gas.
Question 12

The most electronegative element is:

  • A. Oxygen
  • B. Chlorine
  • C. Fluorine
  • D. Nitrogen
Explanation: Fluorine has the highest Pauling electronegativity (3.98). Its small size and high nuclear charge give it the greatest pull on bonding electrons.
Question 13

Graphite conducts electricity because:

  • A. It has ions
  • B. It has free electrons
  • C. It is ionic
  • D. It is metallic
Explanation: Each carbon in graphite forms 3 covalent bonds, leaving one delocalised electron per atom. These free electrons move through the layers, carrying charge.
Question 14

First ionization energy increases across a period due to:

  • A. Decreasing nuclear charge
  • B. Increasing shielding
  • C. Increasing nuclear attraction
  • D. Increasing size
Explanation: Nuclear charge increases across a period while shielding stays roughly constant. Greater nuclear attraction makes it harder to remove the outermost electron.
Question 15

Which compound shows hydrogen bonding?

  • A. HCl
  • B. NH₃
  • C. CH₄
  • D. CO₂
Explanation: Hydrogen bonding requires H bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, or F). NH₃ has H–N bonds with a sufficiently electronegative N. HCl forms only dipole interactions; CH₄ and CO₂ have no H-bond donors.
Question 16

Haber process produces:

  • A. HNO₃
  • B. NH₃
  • C. SO₂
  • D. CO₂
Explanation: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) — The Haber process synthesises ammonia at ~450°C, 150–200 atm, using an iron catalyst.
Question 17

Nitric acid is:

  • A. Weak acid
  • B. Strong oxidizing agent
  • C. Base
  • D. Neutral
Explanation: Concentrated HNO₃ is a strong oxidising agent — it oxidises most metals (except Au and Pt) and many non-metals, producing NO₂ or NO gas.
Question 18

Sulphuric acid acts as:

  • A. Only acid
  • B. Only oxidizing agent
  • C. Dehydrating agent
  • D. Both B and C
Explanation: Concentrated H₂SO₄ is both an oxidising agent (oxidises Cu, S) and a dehydrating agent (removes water from compounds, chars sugar).
Question 19

In VSEPR theory, lone pairs:

  • A. Occupy less space
  • B. Repel more strongly than bond pairs
  • C. Repel less strongly than bond pairs
  • D. Do not affect shape
Explanation: Lone pairs sit closer to the central nucleus, occupying more angular space. They exert greater repulsion than bonding pairs, distorting bond angles (e.g., water: 104.5° instead of 109.5°).
Question 20

Which molecule is non-polar?

  • A. NH₃
  • B. H₂O
  • C. CO₂
  • D. SO₂
Explanation: CO₂ is linear (O=C=O). Each C=O bond is polar, but the two dipoles point in opposite directions and cancel, giving a net dipole of zero. NH₃, H₂O, and SO₂ all have asymmetric shapes with net dipole moments.
Question 21

The hybridization in benzene is:

  • A. sp³
  • B. sp²
  • C. sp
  • D. dsp²
Explanation: Each carbon in benzene forms 3 sigma bonds using sp² hybrid orbitals. The remaining unhybridised p orbital on each carbon overlaps to form the delocalized π system.
Question 22

Which is the strongest acid?

  • A. CH₃COOH
  • B. HCl
  • C. NH₄⁺
  • D. H₂O
Explanation: HCl is a strong acid — it fully dissociates in water. CH₃COOH is a weak acid (partial dissociation), NH₄⁺ is a very weak acid, and H₂O is neutral.
Question 23

The bond dissociation energy of F₂ is lower than Cl₂ due to:

  • A. Larger atomic size
  • B. Strong shielding
  • C. Lone pair repulsion
  • D. High electronegativity
Explanation: F atoms are very small, so lone pairs on the two F atoms in F₂ are very close and repel strongly. This lone pair–lone pair repulsion weakens the F–F bond, giving it a lower dissociation energy than Cl–Cl.
Question 24

Which halogen is most reactive?

  • A. Cl₂
  • B. Br₂
  • C. I₂
  • D. F₂
Explanation: Fluorine is the most reactive halogen — highest electronegativity, smallest atomic radius, and greatest ability to attract electrons. It oxidises almost every other element.
Question 25

The color of bromine is:

  • A. Pale green
  • B. Reddish-brown
  • C. Violet
  • D. Colorless
Explanation: Bromine (Br₂) is a reddish-brown liquid at room temperature. (Chlorine = pale yellow-green; Iodine = dark grey solid / violet vapour; Fluorine = pale yellow gas.)
Question 26

Down Group 17, boiling points:

  • A. Decrease
  • B. Increase
  • C. Remain constant
  • D. Fluctuate
Explanation: Larger atoms down Group 17 have more electrons, increasing London dispersion forces. Stronger intermolecular forces require more energy to overcome → higher boiling points.
Question 27

The strongest reducing agent among halide ions is:

  • A. F⁻
  • B. Cl⁻
  • C. Br⁻
  • D. I⁻
Explanation: I⁻ has the largest ionic radius and most polarisable electron cloud. Its outermost electrons are most loosely held and most easily donated. Reducing power: F⁻ < Cl⁻ < Br⁻ < I⁻.
Question 28

Which halogen forms the strongest hydrogen bond?

  • A. HF
  • B. HCl
  • C. HBr
  • D. HI
Explanation: Fluorine is the most electronegative element, so H–F creates the strongest hydrogen bonds — hence HF has an anomalously high boiling point despite its low molecular mass.
Question 29

Displacement reaction occurs when:

  • A. Less reactive halogen displaces more reactive
  • B. More reactive halogen displaces less reactive
  • C. Same halogens react
  • D. No reaction occurs
Explanation: A more reactive halogen displaces a less reactive halide from solution. Example: Cl₂ + 2KBr → 2KCl + Br₂. Reactivity order: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂.
Question 30

Chlorine reacts with water to form:

  • A. HCl only
  • B. HCl and HOCl
  • C. H₂O₂
  • D. Cl₂O
Explanation: Cl₂ + H₂O ⇌ HCl + HOCl — chlorine disproportionates in water forming hydrochloric acid and hypochlorous acid. HOCl is responsible for the bleaching action of chlorine water.

Question 31 — Equilibrium & Pressure

4 Marks

Consider the following equilibrium reactions:

Reaction I 3Fe(s) + 4H₂O(g) ⇌ Fe₃O₄(s) + 4H₂(g)
Reaction II CO(g) + Cl₂(g) ⇌ COCl₂(g)

State, giving reasons, how an increase in pressure affects the position of equilibrium in each reaction.

Answer

Reaction I

Moles of gas on the left = 4 (H₂O only; Fe and Fe₃O₄ are solids and excluded).
Moles of gas on the right = 4 (H₂ only).

Since the number of moles of gas is equal on both sides, an increase in pressure has no effect on the position of equilibrium.

Reaction II

Moles of gas on the left = 2 (CO + Cl₂).
Moles of gas on the right = 1 (COCl₂).

An increase in pressure shifts the equilibrium to the right (forward direction). By Le Chatelier's principle, the system favours the side with fewer moles of gas to reduce the pressure. More COCl₂ is produced.

Question 32 — Mass Spectrometer

8 Marks

Explain the main stages involved in a mass spectrometer and describe its applications.

Answer

Stages of a Mass Spectrometer

  1. Vaporisation — The sample is heated to convert it into gaseous form so individual atoms/molecules can be processed.
  2. Ionisation — The gaseous sample is bombarded by high-energy electrons, knocking out electrons to form positive ions:
    X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻
  3. Acceleration — Positive ions are accelerated through an electric field. All ions receive the same kinetic energy.
  4. Deflection — Ions pass through a magnetic field, deflected according to their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). Lighter/more-charged ions deflect more.
  5. Detection — Ions hit the detector, generating an electric current proportional to the number of ions. Signals are recorded as a mass spectrum.

Applications

  • Determining relative atomic masses from isotope patterns
  • Identifying isotopes and their relative abundances
  • Determining molecular mass and structural formula of unknown compounds
  • Forensic science — detecting drugs, poisons, or explosives in trace amounts
  • Medical diagnostics — identifying metabolites in blood or urine
  • Carbon dating — measuring ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio to date organic samples
  • Environmental monitoring — detecting pollutants and contaminants

Question 34 — Chemical Bonding

10 Marks
Answer

(a) Define ionic bond

An ionic bond is a strong electrostatic force of attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by the complete transfer of one or more electrons from a metal atom (cation) to a non-metal atom (anion).

Example: Na + Cl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl

(b) Why ionic compounds have high melting points

Ionic compounds form a giant ionic lattice where every positive ion is surrounded by negative ions and vice versa. Strong electrostatic forces act in all directions. Melting requires overcoming all these forces simultaneously — demanding a very large amount of energy, hence high melting points.

(c) Why metals have high melting points

Metals consist of positive cations in a giant metallic lattice surrounded by a sea of delocalised electrons. Strong electrostatic attraction between cations and electrons acts in all directions. Breaking this lattice requires a large amount of energy.

(d) Why silicon dioxide has a high melting point

SiO₂ has a giant covalent network structure — each Si atom is bonded to 4 oxygen atoms by strong covalent bonds in a 3D lattice. Melting requires simultaneously breaking a very large number of strong covalent bonds, requiring enormous energy. Melting point ~1710°C.

(e) Why water has a higher boiling point than H₂S

PropertyH₂OH₂S
Electronegativity of central atomO = 3.44 (very high)S = 2.58 (moderate)
Intermolecular forcesHydrogen bondsWeak van der Waals / dipole-dipole
Boiling point100°C−60°C

Water molecules form hydrogen bonds (O–H···O) because oxygen is highly electronegative and small. These are significantly stronger than the weak forces in H₂S, requiring more energy to separate water molecules during boiling.

(b) Ionization energy

DirectionTrendReason
Across a period →IncreasesGreater nuclear charge; same shielding; electrons harder to remove
Down a group ↓DecreasesElectrons in higher shells, further from nucleus; more inner shells shield nuclear charge
Note: Small dips occur at Group 2→13 (p subshell easier than s) and Group 5→6 (pairing forces destabilise one electron).

(c) Reactivity down Group 17 (Halogens)

Reactivity decreases down the group. Halogens react by gaining an electron. As you descend Group 17, atomic radius increases, shielding increases, effective nuclear charge decreases, and electron affinity decreases — making it harder to attract an incoming electron.

Reactivity: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂

(d) Thermal stability of Group 2 carbonates

MCO₃(s) → MO(s) + CO₂(g)

Thermal stability increases down Group 2: MgCO₃ < CaCO₃ < SrCO₃ < BaCO₃

Smaller, high-charge-density cations (Mg²⁺) polarise the CO₃²⁻ ion more, weakening C–O bonds and making decomposition easier. Larger cations (Ba²⁺) polarise less → more energy needed → higher thermal stability.

(e) The inert pair effect

The inert pair effect is the tendency of the two electrons in the outermost s-subshell to remain non-bonding in heavier p-block elements. The s-electrons become more stable and harder to ionise due to poor shielding by inner d and f electrons. Elements show oxidation states 2 lower than the group maximum:

  • Tl (Group 13): Tl⁺ more stable than Tl³⁺
  • Pb (Group 14): Pb²⁺ more stable than Pb⁴⁺
  • Bi (Group 15): Bi³⁺ more stable than Bi⁵⁺

(b) The Contact Process

The Contact process manufactures sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄):

Stage 1 — Burning sulphur S(s) + O₂(g) → SO₂(g)
Stage 2 — Oxidation of SO₂ (key step) — 450°C, 1–2 atm, V₂O₅ catalyst 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g)
Stage 3 — Absorption in concentrated H₂SO₄ (forms oleum) H₂SO₄ + SO₃ → H₂S₂O₇
Stage 4 — Dilution of oleum H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄

(c) Environmental Issue

The process releases sulphur dioxide (SO₂) into the atmosphere. SO₂ dissolves in rainwater forming acid rain (H₂SO₃/H₂SO₄), which destroys forests, aquatic ecosystems, corrodes structures, and causes respiratory problems.

Question 37 — Electronic Configuration & Periodic Table

6 Marks
Answer

(a) Position in the periodic table

  • The period number = highest principal quantum number (n) of occupied orbitals. Example: Na = [Ne] 3s¹ → n = 3 → Period 3
  • The group number = number of valence electrons. Groups 1–2 are s-block; Groups 13–18 are p-block. Example: Na (1 valence e⁻) → Group 1; Cl (7 valence e⁻) → Group 17
  • The block (s, p, d, f) is determined by the subshell receiving the last (differentiating) electron.

(b) Chemical properties

  • Elements with the same number of valence electrons show similar chemical properties
  • Group 1 (1 valence e⁻): readily lose it → M⁺ ions; highly reactive metals
  • Group 17 (7 valence e⁻): readily gain one → X⁻ ions; reactive non-metals (oxidising agents)
  • Group 18 (full outer shell): chemically inert noble gases
  • Transition metals: incomplete d-subshells → variable oxidation states, coloured compounds, catalytic properties

Question 39 — Halogen Reactivity

5 Marks
Answer

(a) Why F₂ is more reactive than Cl₂ despite weaker F–F bond

Reactivity depends on the overall ability to oxidise (gain electrons), not solely on bond energy. Despite the weak F–F bond (158 kJ/mol vs. 243 kJ/mol for Cl–Cl), fluorine is more reactive because:

  1. Highest electronegativity — F attracts electrons far more strongly than Cl
  2. High electron affinity — large energy release when F gains an electron
  3. High hydration enthalpy of F⁻ — small F⁻ releases large energy when hydrated in aqueous reactions
  4. The overall energy cycle (bond breaking − electron affinity − hydration) is far more thermodynamically favourable for F₂

(b) Why I⁻ is a stronger reducing agent despite I₂ being least reactive

AspectI₂ (halogen)I⁻ (halide ion)
RoleOxidising agent (gains e⁻)Reducing agent (donates e⁻)
TrendLeast reactive halogen — lowest electron affinityStrongest reducing agent — most loosely held electrons
ReasonLarge radius, high shielding → poor ability to attract electronsLarge radius, well-shielded outer electrons → easiest to oxidise
Reducing power of halide ions: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻ (opposite to halogen oxidising power)
