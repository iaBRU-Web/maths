GROUP 18 ELEMENTS (NOBLE GASES)

13.1 — What Are Noble Gases?

Group 18 consists of 6 elements: Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar), Krypton (Kr), Xenon (Xe), and Radon (Rn).

They all have completely filled valence shells — He has 1s², the rest have ns²np⁶. This full outer shell is called the octet structure, which gives them great stability.

Because their outer shells are full, noble gases do not need to gain, lose, or share electrons. This makes them very unreactive — that is why they are called noble gases.

13.2 — Physical Properties

  • All noble gases are monoatomic (exist as single atoms).
  • They are colourless, odourless, and tasteless.
  • Sparingly (slightly) soluble in water.
  • Very low melting and boiling points.
  • Very high ionization energies (due to stable electron configuration).
  • Ionization energy DECREASES ↓ going down the group as atomic size increases.
  • Helium has the lowest boiling point of any known substance: about −269°C.

13.3 — Occurrence

  • All noble gases except radon are found in the atmosphere. Total abundance is 0.03%; argon is the most abundant.
  • Helium and neon are also found in minerals of radioactive origin (e.g. pitchblende, monazite).
  • Radon is obtained from the radioactive decay of Radium-226:
²²⁶Ra → ²²²Rn + ⁴He
  • Xenon and Radon are the rarest elements in the group.

13.4 — Reactivity / Inertness

Noble gases were once called inert gases. However, it was discovered in 1962 by Neil Bartlett that xenon can react with very strong oxidising agents.

Only Krypton and Xenon have been shown to form compounds. Key examples:

Xe + PtF₆ → XePtF₆    (first noble gas compound ever made)
Xe + 2F₂ → XeF₄    (at 400°C, 6 atm, in a nickel vessel)
  • XeF₂, XeF₄, XeF₆ — colourless crystalline solids; powerful fluorinating agents.
  • XeO₃ is a highly explosive xenon oxide.
  • KrF₂ and KrF₄ are known krypton compounds.

These fluorides are easily hydrolysed by water:

2XeF₂ + 2H₂O → 2Xe + 4HF + O₂
2XeF₆ + 3H₂O → XeO₃ + 6HF
Why not He or Ne? Their very small size and very high ionization energies make bond formation almost impossible.

13.5 — Uses of Noble Gases

GasUses

PERIOD 3 ELEMENTS — PROPERTIES & TRENDS

Period 3 elements (left to right): Na, Mg, Al, Si, P, S, Cl, Ar

14.1 — Physical Properties Across Period 3

A) First Ionization Energy

General trend: INCREASES → from Na to Ar. More protons → stronger nuclear attraction → harder to remove an electron.

Two exceptions:

  • Al is lower than Mg — because Al⁺ forms a more stable full 3s² orbital after losing its 3p¹ electron.
  • S is lower than P — because S⁺ forms a more stable half-full 3p³ (each orbital singly filled), whereas S has a paired electron in 3p that repels.

B) Atomic Radius

DECREASES → from Na to Ar. More protons added but electrons enter the same shell (no extra shielding) → stronger pull toward nucleus → smaller atom.

C) Electronegativity

INCREASES → from Na to Cl (Ar has zero — it doesn't form bonds). Stronger nuclear charge and smaller atomic radius → atoms attract shared electrons more strongly.

D) Melting and Boiling Points

The trend is not simple — it depends on the type of bonding:

  • Na, Mg, Al — metals with metallic bonding. Melting point increases (Na < Mg < Al) because the number of delocalized electrons and ionic charge increases (+1, +2, +3).
  • Si — giant covalent structure (like diamond). Very high melting point (~1683 K) — many strong covalent bonds to break.
  • P, S, Cl, Ar — simple molecular. Low melting points because only weak van der Waals forces act between molecules.
  • Order among simple molecules: S₈ > P₄ > Cl₂ > Ar (larger molecule = stronger van der Waals forces).

E) Electrical Conductivity

Na < Mg < Al (increases) — more delocalized electrons as we go from Na to Al. Si is a semiconductor. P, S, Cl, Ar = zero conductivity (electrons are in covalent bonds or stable shells, not free to move).

F) Metallic Character & Redox

Metallic character DECREASES → left to right. Non-metallic character increases. As a result:

  • Reducing power DECREASES left to right (metals on the left lose electrons easily).
  • Oxidising power INCREASES left to right (non-metals on the right gain electrons easily).

G) Electron Affinity

Generally becomes more negative (more energy released) going left to right — atoms more readily accept electrons as nuclear charge increases.

  • Exception: Mg has positive EA (adding an electron forces it into the less stable 3p level from the full 3s²).
  • Exception: P has less negative EA than expected (half-filled 3p³ is particularly stable).

14.2 — Chemical Properties of Period 3 Elements

Reaction with Water

  • Na: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂  (vigorous with cold water — alkaline solution)
  • Mg: Mg + 2H₂O → Mg(OH)₂ + H₂  (slow with cold water; fast with steam → MgO + H₂)
  • Al: 2Al + 6H₂O → 2Al(OH)₃ + 3H₂  (very slow with steam only — protected by oxide layer)
  • Si: Si + 2H₂O → SiO₂ + 2H₂  (reacts with steam at very high temperature)
  • P, S: No reaction with water.
  • Cl: Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HOCl  (dissolves to give acidic solution; HOCl slowly decomposes in sunlight)

Reaction with Oxygen

  • Na: 4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O  (white ionic solid)
  • Mg: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO  (burns brilliantly white)
  • Al: 4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃  (slow surface coating of oxide)
  • Si: Si + O₂ → SiO₂  (giant covalent structure)
  • P (white): P₄ + 5O₂ → P₄O₁₀  (ignites spontaneously — stored under water)
  • S: S + O₂ → SO₂  (burns with lilac/blue flame)

Reaction with NaOH

  • Na, Mg: No reaction.
  • Al: 2Al + 2NaOH + 6H₂O → 2NaAl(OH)₄ + 3H₂ — Al is amphoteric (reacts with both acid and base).
  • Cl: Cl₂ + 2NaOH → NaOCl + NaCl + H₂O  (sodium hypochlorite — used in bleach/antiseptics like Jik)
  • P: P₄ + 3OH⁻ + 3H₂O → PH₃ + 3H₂PO₂⁻  (produces phosphine gas)

Reaction with HCl

  • Na: 2Na + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂
  • Mg: Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂
  • Al: 2Al + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂
  • Si, P, S, Cl: No reaction with HCl.

FACTORS AFFECTING CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIUM

15.1 — Reversible vs. Irreversible Reactions

An irreversible (complete) reaction goes in one direction only — reactants are fully converted to products.

NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂

A reversible reaction proceeds in both directions — products can reform reactants. Written with a double arrow (⇌).

2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃
PCl₅ ⇌ PCl₃ + Cl₂
  • Forward reaction → goes left to right
  • Reverse reaction → goes right to left

15.2 — Dynamic Equilibrium

When a reversible reaction occurs in a closed system, it reaches a state called dynamic equilibrium.

Definition
Chemical equilibrium is the state at which the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the backward reaction.

At equilibrium:

  • Concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (but not necessarily equal).
  • Both forward and reverse reactions are still occurring — the system is NOT stopped.
  • The net change is zero.

Types of equilibrium:

  • Homogeneous — all species in the same phase. E.g. N₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2NO(g)
  • Heterogeneous — species in different phases. E.g. CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g)

Characteristics of Dynamic Equilibrium

  • Rate of forward reaction = Rate of reverse reaction.
  • Concentrations remain constant over time.
  • System is closed and temperature is constant.
  • Equilibrium can be reached from either direction (starting with reactants OR products).

15.3 — Le Châtelier's Principle

Le Châtelier
When a system in equilibrium is disturbed (by changing temperature, pressure, or concentration), the equilibrium shifts in the direction that opposes the change and restores balance.

A) Effect of Temperature

Think of heat as a reactant (endothermic) or product (exothermic):

  • Forward reaction is EXOTHERMIC (ΔH < 0): Increasing temperature → shifts ← LEFT (backward). Decreasing temperature → shifts → RIGHT (forward).
  • Forward reaction is ENDOTHERMIC (ΔH > 0): Increasing temperature → shifts → RIGHT (forward). Decreasing temperature → shifts ← LEFT (backward).
Haber Process example: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)   ΔH = −92 kJ/mol
Forward reaction is exothermic → low temperature favours more NH₃ BUT makes the reaction very slow. Compromise: 400–450°C is used.

B) Effect of Concentration

  • Increasing concentration of a reactant → equilibrium shifts → RIGHT (forward) to use up the added reactant.
  • Increasing concentration of a product → equilibrium shifts ← LEFT (backward) to use up the added product.
  • Removing a product → equilibrium shifts → RIGHT to replace it.
  • Removing a reactant → equilibrium shifts ← LEFT.
Example: Fe³⁺(aq) + SCN⁻(aq) ⇌ FeSCN²⁺(aq)  [yellow/orange ⇌ reddish-brown]
Adding more Fe³⁺ or SCN⁻ → solution becomes darker (more FeSCN²⁺ formed, equilibrium shifts right).

C) Effect of Pressure (for gases only)

  • Increasing pressure → equilibrium shifts toward the side with FEWER moles of gas.
  • Decreasing pressure → equilibrium shifts toward the side with MORE moles of gas.
Example: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) → 4 moles gas on left, 2 moles on right.
Increasing pressure → shifts RIGHT → more NH₃ formed (fewer gas molecules).
In the Haber process, high pressure (150–300 atm) is used to get a good yield of NH₃.

D) Effect of a Catalyst

A catalyst DOES NOT shift the equilibrium position. It speeds up BOTH the forward and reverse reactions equally.

  • The system reaches equilibrium faster, but the equilibrium concentrations remain the same.
  • In the Haber process: Iron (Fe) catalyst is used.
  • In the Contact process: V₂O₅ (vanadium pentoxide) catalyst is used.
